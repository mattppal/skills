# The barbell strategy for vibe coding

I've noticed something about working with AI for code: the middle ground is the worst place to be.

You know that awkward space where you're sort of watching, sort of waiting, sort of engaged? You're not hands-on enough to stay in flow state, but not hands-off enough to context switch to something else.

That's dead time.

The solution is a barbell strategy: go to the extremes.

## The two modes that actually work

**Rapid iteration mode**

This is where you treat Replit like a design partner. Low/Medium Autonomy with the queue feature. You're firing off continuous small requests, testing in parallel, staying in complete control.

You're in flow state. Every change is small enough to evaluate instantly. Your brain never leaves "testing and refining" mode.

This is the mode for dialing things in exactly how you want them. Polish. Tweaks. Getting the details right.

**Hands-off mode**

This is the opposite extreme. High/Max Autonomy with Self-testing give the AI a clear goal and step away completely.

Go make coffee. Take a walk. Work on something else. Come back when it's done.

No hovering. No watching progress bars. No wondering if you should interrupt.

This is the mode for substantial features. "Build a user authentication system with email verification." "Add a dark mode toggle that persists across sessions." "Implement search with filters."

The AI works through the problem systematically while you do something that actually requires your attention.

## Why the middle fails

The middle ground is where most people get stuck. You're engaged enough that you can't focus on anything else, but not engaged enough to add real value.

You're watching the AI work. Waiting for it to finish so you can course-correct. Not sure if you should interrupt or let it continue.

That's not flow state. That's not productive waiting. It's just friction.

Your brain is in "monitoring" mode, which is the least useful state for creative work. You're not making decisions. You're not testing. You're not thinking about something else. You're just... watching.

## How to choose

The decision is simpler than it seems.

**Use rapid iteration when:**
- You know exactly what you want
- The changes are refinements, not foundations
- You're in the "make it exactly right" phase
- Testing requires your judgment

**Use hands-off when:**
- You can articulate the goal clearly
- The implementation details don't matter to you
- You have something else productive to do
- The feature is substantial enough to warrant context switching

The key is commitment. Pick an extreme and commit to it. Don't hover in the middle hoping to get the benefits of both.

## What this looks like in practice

I'm building a dashboard. I need a data visualization component and some polish on the existing UI.

For the data visualization, I switch to High Autonomy. "Build a line chart component using Chart.js that displays monthly revenue. Include hover states, a legend, and responsive sizing." Then I close that window and work on documentation for twenty minutes.

When I come back, the component is there. Maybe it needs adjustments, but the foundation is solid.

For the UI polish, I switch to Low Autonomy. I'm testing the app, queuing up tweaks. "Button padding feels tight." "Move that label up 5px." "Darker border on the input fields."

I'm in flow state for fifteen minutes, making continuous tiny improvements until everything feels right.

Two different modes for two different types of work. No time spent in the awkward middle.

## The productivity unlock

The barbell strategy isn't just about efficiency. It's about cognitive clarity.

When you're in rapid iteration mode, you know you're steering. Full attention, full control.

When you're in hands-off mode, you know you're delegating. No attention, no monitoring.

No confusion about what your role is. No wasted attention on watching progress bars.

That clarity is what enables flow state on one end and productive context switching on the other.

Avoid the middle. Pick an extreme. Commit to it.

That's when AI-assisted development actually feels like it's making you faster instead of just giving you something new to manage.
