**b2b vs b2c**

- b2c
    
    I disagree with the author here. B2C posting is a learned skill, not something you either have or don't have.
    
    Most skills are pattern matching—you see what communication works in a given medium, map that to your own experiences, and communicate in ways people understand.
    
    You can absolutely learn B2C posting, but learning is 'cringe.' That said, there's an optimal, non-zero amount of cringe.
    
    Being a little cringe is actually good—it stirs up conversation and helps your development. Don't worry too much about what people think. I'm speaking in Portugal this week in front of 300 people and just riffing. That works because I've practiced and I'm confident in my knowledge. I'm not very concerned with what they'll think of me.
    
    Granted, I've been doing this for years, but you'll need to learn to speak publicly or be confident in what you're saying as founders.
    
    Counterpoint: if B2C isn't your thing and you don't feel like you can riff, that's fine. You can stick to purely educational content—that's incredibly well respected.
    
- b2b
    
    I agree with the author—B2B content is the highest value you can get.
    
    It may not go viral or get you the most followers (though well-executed B2B content can), but it builds respect among the people who matter.
    
    See the [attached Palmer Luckey video](https://x.com/lulumeservey/status/1970837275249447419) for more on building an audience with the right people.
    
    The author is spot on:
    
    > You're going to be learning new things constantly - make writing about it a natural conversion point for things you learn. Learn to write well, you learn to think well, and you'll naturally become a better storyteller for all the parts of the puzzle by constantly trying them out on an audience and seeing how well you do.
    > 
    
    B2B content is just building in public!
    
    Unfortunately, this is also directionally correct:
    
    > Hype = ( Potential Impact ) x ( Probability of Happening ) x ( Clarity of Explanation )
    > 
    
    That's why everything about Cursor / Claude code blows up. The content isn't that great, but it's already happening and having a big impact 😕
    
    I've benefitted from this at Replit.
    
    It's not fair, but you can manipulate the other variables. When Browserbase posts browser automation content that blows up, or Gemini or Browser Use, look at that as a signal for potential impact or other variables influencing virality.
    
    This is what I mean when I say we can engage with this type of content to get into the popular discourse.
    
    Similarly, Cursor and Claude are very popular—you can draw parallels between your company and these technologies to generate hype.

**How to Use Twitter**

I agree with the author. Think from the viewer's perspective—people want to engage or reshare content that's entertaining, insightful, or makes them look good.

There's nothing wrong with starting as a reply guy. You can make meaningful connections by commenting genuinely and engaging in discussion. *Especially for small accounts*, this is a great way to kickstart growth (and make friends)

Replying to big accounts is also a great way to get tons of impressions as a new account. If you're one of the first responders to a post that eventually hits a million views and you have a top reply, that can generate hundreds of thousands of impressions.

Charts, graphs, dramatic captions, and infographics do incredibly well. See the last lesson for examples.

Quote tweeting gets engagement boosted at the moment. Adding your own takes can perform surprisingly well.
